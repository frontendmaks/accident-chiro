Cervicogenic Headache vs. True Concussion Headache

The most important diagnostic challenge after a crash is distinguishing between cervicogenic headaches (caused by cervical spine injury) and true concussion headaches (caused by brain trauma). They present similarly but require completely different treatment. Our comprehensive assessment differentiates between the two — and treats both effectively. Cervicogenic headaches respond dramatically to cervical spine manipulation and soft tissue work.

😵

Vestibular Dysfunction — Dizziness After Crashes

The vestibular system (inner ear + brainstem + cerebellum) is uniquely vulnerable to crash forces. Vestibular concussion presents as dizziness, vertigo, nausea with head movement, and balance problems. Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) — when otoconia crystals are dislodged by the crash — is the most common and most treatable form. Our Epley maneuver protocol resolves BPPV in most patients within 1–3 sessions.

👁️

Visual and Oculomotor Disturbance

Post-concussion visual problems — light sensitivity, difficulty tracking, eye-convergence issues — indicate damage to the oculomotor pathways. These symptoms cause significant functional impairment: difficulty reading, screen sensitivity, inability to drive safely. We assess and refer for specialized neuro-optometric rehabilitation when oculomotor involvement is identified.

🌡️

Autonomic Nervous System Dysregulation

Severe crashes can disrupt the autonomic nervous system's regulation of heart rate, blood pressure, and temperature. Post-traumatic dysautonomia presents as fatigue, brain fog, exercise intolerance, and heart palpitations. Upper cervical adjustments — particularly C1-C2 — have demonstrated effectiveness in normalizing autonomic function disrupted by cervical trauma.

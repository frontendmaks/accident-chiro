The Most Common Back Injuries in Oregon Car Accidents

Car accidents generate forces that your lumbar spine was not designed to absorb. Here's what our Portland specialists actually find and treat — not a generic list, but the specific injuries we diagnose in crash patients every week.

💧

Disc Herniation (L4–L5, L5–S1)

The L4–L5 and L5–S1 discs are the most commonly herniated in crash patients. Crash forces compress the disc, forcing the soft nucleus material to push through the fibrous outer ring. If this contacts the sciatic nerve root, you get radiating pain down the leg — classic sciatica. Our treatment protocol decompresses affected discs and reduces nerve pressure without surgery.

Radiating leg pain Sciatica Numbness/tingling
🔧

Lumbar Facet Syndrome

Each lumbar vertebra has four facet joints — small joints that guide and limit spinal movement. In crashes, rapid compression forces impact these joints, causing cartilage damage and synovial inflammation. Facet syndrome presents as deep, aching lower back pain that worsens with extension and rotation. Highly treatable with targeted manipulation and anti-inflammatory soft tissue work.

Deep back aching Worsens with extension
💪

Lumbar Sprain/Strain Complex

The posterior longitudinal ligaments and the multifidus/erector spinae muscles are often simultaneously sprained and strained in crashes. This combined injury is particularly debilitating because ligaments heal slowly (they have limited blood supply) and the muscle weakness that results leads to secondary spasm and guarding that perpetuates the pain cycle for months without treatment.

Muscle spasm Stiffness on waking
⚡

Compression Fractures (T12–L2)

In higher-force crashes — especially those involving rollovers or front-end impacts where the body is braced against a seatbelt — compression fractures of the thoracolumbar junction (T12–L2) can occur. These are often missed on initial ER X-rays and discovered weeks later. If you have sharp pain on spinal percussion after a significant crash, this must be ruled out before any manipulation.

Our Back Injury Protocol

Evidence-Based Treatment for Crash-Induced Spine Injuries

Lumbar decompression — creating negative disc pressure to retract herniated material.

Core rehabilitation — rebuilding lumbar stabilizers that atrophy after disc injury.

1

Structural Assessment — Find the Real Injury

Lumbar and thoracic range of motion, orthopedic tests (SLR, slump test, Kemp's), motor strength testing, and reflexes. Determines whether you need imaging referral (MRI for disc herniation) before any treatment begins.

2

Spinal Decompression Therapy

Mechanical traction that creates controlled negative intradiscal pressure — allowing herniated material to retract and reducing nerve compression without surgery. Highly effective for L4–L5 and L5–S1 disc herniations from crash impact.

3

Manipulation & Soft Tissue Work

Grade III–IV lumbar mobilization for facet syndrome, combined with targeted soft tissue work for the multifidus, quadratus lumborum, and piriformis — reducing the reflex muscle guarding that perpetuates back pain after injury.

4

Progressive Core Rehabilitation

Staged core stabilization starting with transversus abdominis activation, progressing through functional movement patterns. Patients who complete our rehabilitation program have dramatically lower rates of chronic back pain recurrence than those who only receive passive treatment.
